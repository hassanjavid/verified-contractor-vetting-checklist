Hiring a contractor for a home improvement project is one of the most consequential financial decisions a homeowner can make. According to recent survey data, more than half (52%) of American homeowners have a renovation project planned, with median budgets hovering around $15,000. Yet home improvement fraud remains one of the most common consumer complaints filed with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), frequently involving unlicensed or uninsured contractors.

This disconnect between homeowner investment and contractor reliability underscores a simple truth: finding a verified contractor is not a luxury - it is a financial necessity. A verified contractor offers documented proof of licensing, insurance, bonding, and a track record of satisfied customers. Without such verification, homeowners risk shoddy workmanship, safety violations, budget overruns, and even legal liability.

This comprehensive vetting checklist draws on guidance from the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), the Better Business Bureau (BBB), state licensing boards, and industry organizations to help you identify and hire a verified contractor you can trust.

Step 1: Verify Licensing with Your State Licensing Board

The cornerstone of any verified contractor evaluation is a valid, active state license. Licensing requirements vary by state, but a legitimate contractor working on projects above a certain dollar threshold must typically hold a license appropriate to the scope of work.

How to verify:
  • Ask the contractor for their license number and full business name exactly as registered
  • Search your state's official licensing database online - most states provide free public access through portals like Arkansas.gov, Oklahoma.gov, or the California Contractors State License Board (CSLB)
  • Confirm the license is "Active" and covers the specific type of work you need (e.g., a "Limited" license may only cover projects up to a certain dollar amount)
  • Verify that the business name on the license matches the name on the contract

The FTC advises homeowners to search contractors' company names alongside terms like "complaint" or "scam" to uncover any negative history. A verified contractor will have a clean licensing record and will not hesitate to provide this information.

Step 2: Confirm Insurance Coverage for Liability and Workers' Compensation

Insurance verification is non-negotiable. Without proper coverage, the homeowner may be held financially responsible for accidents, injuries, or property damage that occur during the project. The FTC recommends that contractors carry personal liability, workers' compensation, and property damage coverage.

How to verify:
  • Request a Certificate of Insurance (COI) directly from the contractor
  • The COI should show General Liability Insurance (to cover damage to your home) and Workers' Compensation Insurance (to cover the contractor's crew)
  • Call the insurance carrier listed on the certificate to confirm the policy is active and matches the contractor's legal business entity
  • A verified contractor will have no issue with you contacting their insurer directly

Industry standards suggest general liability coverage of at least $1 million per occurrence for residential projects. If the contractor uses subcontractors, ask for proof that those subcontractors are also properly insured.

Step 3: Check for Bonding to Protect Against Incomplete Work

A surety bond is an additional layer of protection that many homeowners overlook. A bond ensures that if the contractor fails to complete the job, fails to pay for permits, or violates licensing laws, the bonding company may compensate the homeowner up to the bond amount.

What to look for:
  • Ask the contractor if they are bonded and for what amount
  • Verify the bond with the surety company that issued it
  • Understand that bond requirements vary by state and project type - some states require specific bonds for certain contractor classifications

A truly verified contractor typically carries bonding as part of their professional credentials. While a bond does not guarantee quality work, it provides a financial backstop if the contractor abandons the project.

Step 4: Investigate Complaint History Through the BBB and Other Platforms

Online reviews are helpful but should never be the sole basis for hiring. Fake and incentivized reviews have become increasingly common, which is why the Better Business Bureau (BBB) consistently advises homeowners to verify a contractor's complaint history, not just their star rating.

Research steps:
  • Look up the contractor on BBB.org to see verified reviews, complaint history, BBB ratings, and how the business resolves disputes
  • Check Google Business Profile for a high volume of reviews over a sustained period - beware of a sudden burst of five-star reviews all within the same week
  • Cross-reference specialized review platforms like Angi, Houzz, and Yelp
  • Read complaints carefully: a verified contractor will have few unresolved complaints and a history of responding professionally to issues

The BBB also advises caution if a company is difficult to communicate with or demands full payment upfront - both are red flags that should disqualify a candidate.

Step 5: Obtain and Compare Multiple Written Quotes

The FTC recommends getting written estimates from several firms and advises against automatically choosing the lowest bidder. The BBB suggests obtaining at least three apples-to-apples quotes based on the same specifications, materials, labor, and time factors.

How to evaluate quotes:
  • Ensure each quote includes a detailed scope of work, materials list, timeline, and payment schedule
  • Compare quotes based on the same requirements - do not compare a comprehensive bid to a bare-bones estimate
  • Be wary of bids that are significantly lower than others, as extremely low bids may indicate cutting corners, subpar materials, or hidden costs that will surface later
  • A verified contractor will provide transparency in pricing and a clear, written breakdown of all costs

The National Association of the Remodeling Industry advises making sure you are comparing "apples to apples" and considering factors like quality, timeline, and scope alongside price.

Step 6: Demand a Written Contract with Key Protections

Never permit work to start without a written contract. The BBB warns that a lack of written agreement is a major red flag. A proper contract protects both parties and should include:

Essential contract elements:
  • Detailed scope of work describing exactly what will be done
  • Start and completion dates for each phase of the project
  • Exact costs, including materials, labor, permits, and any contingencies
  • Payment schedule that ties payments to completed work milestones, not arbitrary dates
  • Warranty information covering both materials and workmanship
  • Responsibility for obtaining permits and scheduling inspections
  • A clause stating all changes must be in writing and signed by both parties before work proceeds

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) also warns against large upfront payments. Experts recommend limiting deposits to less than 10-30 percent, staggering payments according to work stages, and withholding the final payment until the job is fully completed and all liens are released.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: What is the difference between a licensed contractor and a verified contractor?

A licensed contractor has met the minimum legal requirements to perform certain types of work in a specific state. A verified contractor goes beyond licensing by also confirming valid insurance, bonding, a clean complaint history with the BBB, positive references from recent clients, and appropriate licensing for the specific project at hand.

Q2: How many contractors should I interview before making a decision?

Industry experts recommend scheduling in-person interviews with at least three contractors who specialize in the type of work you need. The FTC suggests obtaining written estimates from several firms, comparing them side by side before making a final selection.

Q3: Is it safe to hire a contractor who does not require a deposit?

Some reputable contractors do work on a "pay as you go" basis with no deposit, but this is not universal. What matters is the structure of payments - not whether a deposit exists. A verified contractor will never demand large upfront payments. Stagger payments according to work completion and never pay the full balance until the job is finished and you have inspected the work.

Q4: Can I verify a contractor's license and insurance myself for free?

Yes. Every state provides free public access to contractor licensing databases through official government websites. You can also request a Certificate of Insurance directly from the contractor and call the insurance carrier to verify coverage. The BBB offers free business profiles that include complaint history and ratings. These verification steps cost nothing but time and provide essential protection.

Q5: What should I do if I suspect I have hired an unverified contractor?

Stop all work immediately if you have serious concerns about licensing or insurance. Verify the contractor's credentials through your state licensing board - if they are unlicensed, they are likely operating illegally. Document all communications, payments, and work performed. Contact your state's attorney general office and file a complaint with the BBB. In cases of fraud or abandonment, consult a qualified attorney to explore legal options.
